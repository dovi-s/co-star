This is a deep look at co-star's most direct competitor model. Here's what I'm pulling:

WeAudition is the incumbent, but it's a fundamentally different product. It connects actors with human readers via video chat. That's its strength and its limitation. The reviews are glowing — actors love the human connection, coaching, and emotional authenticity. But it comes with friction: scheduling, cost, availability, and dependence on another person.

What WeAudition does well that co-star should learn from:

Readers give notes and coaching — not just reading lines. Actors value the feedback loop.
You can "cast your reader" — need a Texan accent? A male reader for a romantic scene? They match it. co-star already does this with Smart Cast voice assignment.
Adjustable reader volume after taping — clever detail for the self-tape workflow.
Casting director endorsements are powerful social proof (NCIS, Black Mirror, Wednesday, Mission Impossible casting directors all quoted).
Where co-star wins over WeAudition:

Instant availability. No scheduling, no waiting, no pulling favors. 24/7 truly means 24/7 with AI.
Free/low cost. WeAudition readers charge fees (some free, many paid). Actors tipping $2-5 per session adds up across 120+ auditions/year.
Privacy. No sharing your audition material with a stranger. No NDA concerns. No human seeing your worst takes.
Speed. Paste script, pick role, go. No explaining the scene to a reader, no setup time.
Consistency. The AI reader delivers reliably every time. No bad readers, no technical issues with video chat.
The positioning nuance: WeAudition proves actors want a scene partner, not just a recording. The human connection and feedback are what they value. co-star should lean into the "scene partner" language rather than "reader" — it's a warmer, more collaborative frame. But co-star shouldn't try to replicate coaching/notes — that's where human readers genuinely add value. co-star's lane is the fast, private, always-available rehearsal partner for when you need to run lines right now.

Here's a summary of everything we've learned from all the research you've shared:

The Market

Self-taping is now the dominant audition method. Working actors are doing 120-164+ auditions per year, mostly self-taped.
The volume is brutal, especially for actors with day jobs. Efficiency is everything — actors are taping at desks at 1am on Fridays.
The reader problem is the #1 pain point. Threads going back 8 years are all asking the same question: "How do I self-tape without a reader?"
The Competition

WeAudition — the incumbent. Connects actors with human readers via video chat. Great reviews, casting director endorsements. But requires scheduling, costs money per session, and depends on another human being available.
ColdRead — most-praised app reader, but iOS only. Responds to cue words, can adjust voice pitch. Actors literally buy cheap iPhones just to use it.
LineLearner — cross-platform but basic. Timed pauses, not cue-responsive.
Rafy, Linus AI, ScenePartnerAI, Acting Pal — newer AI options, mentioned but not deeply adopted yet.
Slatable ($0/$4.99/$9.99) — competitor in the broader audition tool space.
Sides (trysides.com) — AI self-tape feedback tool, universally rejected by actors.
Where co-star Wins

Works everywhere (PWA, not iOS-locked like ColdRead)
Instant, no scheduling, no human dependency
Privacy-first — actors are deeply protective of their material (WeTransfer AI scare, NDA concerns)
Natural conversational voice quality (our recent TTS overhaul)
Zero-setup flow: paste script, pick role, go
The AI Sentiment

Actors are hostile toward AI that judges, replaces, or evaluates their performance. "You're gonna use a computer to tell you if you gave a convincing human performance?" gets upvoted heavily.
But actors quietly accept AI as a practical tool for preparation. People are using AI readers and booking work from them.
The line is clear: AI as a tool for the actor = OK. AI replacing the actor or evaluating the craft = not OK.
co-star's positioning as a rehearsal partner (not an AI product) is exactly right for this climate.
What Actors Care About Technically

Audio quality matters more than video quality. Bad audio is an instant pass from casting directors.
Keep production simple. Plain background, good lighting, good audio. No green screens, no fancy transitions.
Actors expect to be off-book (lines memorized). Script text visible in a submission tape would look amateur.
Multiple takes are standard — some actors submit a dozen. But they manage takes themselves; they just need the reader to be ready again instantly.
The Emotional Dimension

"Self-taping can be a lonely game and sometimes relentlessly unrewarding."
Actors want the process to feel less isolating. A scene partner that's always available and never judgmental addresses a real emotional need.
The best working actors treat the audition itself as the reward — enjoying the process, not just chasing bookings.
Brand Positioning Takeaways

Never lead with "AI." Lead with the outcome: rehearse anytime, anywhere.
The industry accepts AI for preparation but rejects AI replacing human elements.
Privacy and local data storage are genuine differentiators actors will care about.
"Scene partner" language > "reader" language (warmer, more collaborative).
co-star should feel like a tool made by people who understand actors, for actors.

is teleprompting something we want or should have?

Good question. Teleprompter mode is a scrolling text display that shows the full script as you perform, like a news anchor's teleprompter. It's a different approach from our three-line reader.

Our three-line reader is actually more sophisticated for rehearsal - it shows previous, current, and next lines with highlighting, context peek, and memorization modes that progressively hide text. A teleprompter just scrolls text at a set speed.

That said, they serve different use cases. A teleprompter is useful for self-tapes where you want to glance at lines while recording, especially early in the memorization process. Our three-line reader already covers the core need (seeing what's coming next), and our memorization levels (Full, Partial, Cue, Memory) give actors a better path to getting off-book than a teleprompter does.

So it's not a gap - it's a design choice. We could mark it as "partial" in the table since our reader does overlap with what a teleprompter does, or we could just leave it as-is to be honest about what we don't have. What's your preference?
